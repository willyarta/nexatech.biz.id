The Secrets of Blue Zones: Why Some People Live Over 100

Blue Zones are regions in the world where people live significantly longer than the global average, often reaching 100 years or more with high levels of vitality and cognitive clarity.

The Power 9, a set of common denominators shared by these populations, highlights that longevity is less about genetics and more about the environment and consistent daily choices.

A plant-slanted diet is a universal trait among centenarians. Legumes, including black beans, soy, and lentils, are the cornerstone of most centenarian diets, providing high-quality fiber and protein.

Natural movement is built into their daily lives. Instead of hitting the gym, people in Blue Zones garden, walk to the market, or perform manual household tasks that keep them active consistently.

Having a sense of purpose, or 'ikigai' as the Okinawans call it, can add up to seven years of healthy life expectancy. Knowing why you wake up in the morning keeps the spirit resilient.

Low stress is equally important. While they face the same challenges as everyone else, these populations have integrated stress-relieving rituals into their daily routines, such as naps or community gatherings.

Social connectivity is perhaps the strongest predictor of long life. Centenarians are usually part of tight-knit communities where individuals look out for one another and elderly status is celebrated.

Emulating Blue Zone habits doesn't require moving to a remote island. By prioritizing movement, purpose, and plants, anyone can improve their chances of a long and fulfilling life.

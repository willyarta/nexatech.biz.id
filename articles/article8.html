Gut Health: The Secret to Overall Well-being

Your gut is home to trillions of microbes that make up your microbiome. This complex ecosystem is so influential that scientists often refer to the gut as the 'second brain,' directly communicating with the central nervous system.

A healthy gut microbiome is essential for digestion and the absorption of nutrients. Without the right balance of bacteria, your body struggles to break down fibers and synthesize certain vitamins like Vitamin K and B12.

Approximately 70 percent of your immune system is located in your gut. The beneficial bacteria found there help train your immune cells to distinguish between harmless food particles and dangerous pathogens like viruses or harmful bacteria.

The 'gut-brain axis' is the biochemical signaling that takes place between the gastrointestinal tract and the central nervous system. This link explains why stress can cause stomach upset, and why gut issues can contribute to anxiety.

Probiotics and prebiotics are the building blocks of gut health. Probiotics are live beneficial bacteria found in fermented foods, while prebiotics are types of fiber that act as a food source for the bacteria already in your gut.

Antibiotics, while life-saving, can decimate your gut microbiome. They do not distinguish between 'good' and 'bad' bacteria, often requiring weeks or months of targeted nutrition to restore the natural bacterial balance after a course.

Sugar and highly processed foods are the enemies of a healthy gut. They promote the growth of harmful yeast and bacteria that can lead to inflammation, 'leaky gut' syndrome, and a host of systemic health issues like autoimmune disorders.

Focusing on variety in your diet—especially a wide range of colorful plants—is the best way to ensure a diverse and resilient microbiome. A diverse gut is a strong gut, capable of supporting your health in ways we are only beginning to understand.
